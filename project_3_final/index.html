New Hampshire's highest-paid worker? A corrections officer

By Katy Savage

The top-earning state employee in New Hampshire last year wasn’t the governor or a department head — it was a corrections officer. Lieutenant Dwane Sweatt, who works for the New Hampshire Department of Corrections, earned $360,573.14 last year. The majority of that — $272,800.67 — came from overtime alone. According to the data, Sweatt works at least 102 hours a week.

Sweatt's case isn't unusual. Overtime pay within the Department of Corrections cost New Hampshire taxpayers $27.3 million in 2024. And, total employee compensation for the department reached $92.3 million. The department is now running $10.8 million over its current year’s budget—all due to staffing shortages.

In May of this year, Corrections Commissioner Helen Hanks announced her resignation, citing staffing and budget concerns.

Overtime is often mandatory in New Hampshire’s correctional facilities, where officers are frequently required to work beyond their scheduled shifts. Though vacancy rates have dropped slightly — from 52% in January 2024 to 43% in May 2025 — the shortages continue to put pressure on existing staff.

Seifu Ragassa – Corrections Superintendent

Ragassa was the second-highest paid state employee in 2024, earning $330,656.03. His pay included $210,593.32 in overtime, $103,350.02 in regular salary, and $13,012.75 in holiday pay, with an additional $3,699.94 in other compensation.

Union Gains and Pay Bumps

The department has also agreed to significant wage increases, including 10% and 2% raises for all staff, double overtime pay for hours beyond 80 per week, and enhanced holiday pay to entice more workers. These increases, though beneficial for workers, have further strained the department’s budget, according to New Hampshire Public Radio .
